# Human User Guide

## About me

### What are some honest, unfiltered things about you?

- I’m a **builder** at heart; I like understanding how systems fit together, not just using them.
- I care a lot about clarity and maintainability.
- I’m pretty extroverted for a developer and genuinely enjoy bouncing ideas around in real time.
- I get a lot of energy from learning new tools, workflows, and little productivity hacks, then sharing them.
- I have strong opinions about development practices, but I’m willing to change my mind when shown better data or clearer reasoning.

### What drives you nuts?

- Vague requirements and hand‑wavy acceptance criteria where success isn’t clearly defined.
- Cargo‑culting tools or patterns "because that’s what everyone does" without understanding the tradeoffs.
- Overly verbose, tangled code when a cleaner, more readable structure is clearly possible.
- Communication that hides the real issue: passive‑aggressive comments, status updates that say nothing, or burying important problems.
- Process for process’s sake—ceremony that doesn’t improve quality, speed, or reliability.

### What are your quirks?

- I like to chip away at problems iteratively: ship something small, learn, then refine, rather than design in a vacuum for weeks.
- In conflict, I default to "what problem are we solving?" and try to anchor on shared goals instead of taking sides.
- I enjoy deep‑dive technical rabbit holes, but I still want the end result to be pragmatic and shippable.
- I’ll sometimes prototype in a quick, messy way, then insist on a proper cleanup pass before calling it "done."
- When learning something new I prefer video format. I also like to learn just enough to accomplish the task at hand, then dive deeper as needed.

### What are some things that people might misunderstand about you that you should clarify?

- When I push back on an approach, it’s rarely about ego—it’s about maintainability, risk, or long‑term cost.
- My enthusiasm for tools, workflows, and refactors isn’t a criticism of existing work. It’s me trying to make life easier for future us.
- I like to ask questions but I value your time. I'll try to only reach out if the trade is hours of my time for a few minutes of yours.

## About my colleagues

### What qualities do you particularly value in your colleagues?

- Low/no ego.
- Clear thinkers who can explain their reasoning simply and are open to being challenged.
- People who care about code readability, tests, and long‑term maintainability, not just short‑term velocity.
- Colleagues who give early feedback, surface risks promptly, and don’t wait until the last minute to raise concerns.
- Teammates who are curious: they ask "why" about product decisions and implementation details.
- People who are comfortable with async communication but willing to jump on a quick call when something is stuck.

### What do you wish colleagues didn’t do?

- Drop drive‑by comments without context, examples, or suggestions for improvement.
- Sit on blockers or confusion instead of asking for help or clarification.
- Over‑optimize for cleverness or micro‑performance at the expense of clarity and simplicity.
- Treat processes, checklists, or patterns as sacred instead of tools we can adapt.
- Default to large, monolithic changes when smaller, reviewable increments would be safer and easier to reason about.

### How can people earn an extra gold star with you?

- Write concise, well‑structured PR descriptions that explain the "why," not just the "what."
- Pre‑think tradeoffs and come with options, e.g., "Here are two approaches and why I’m leaning toward this one."
- Share a new trick, tool, or workflow that meaningfully improves the dev experience—especially around Git, testing, or automation.
- Offer to pair when something’s hairy instead of tossing it over the wall.

## About my interactions

### How do you coach people to do their best work and develop their talents?

- I like to start by understanding what someone wants to get better at (architecture, debugging, delivery, etc.) and work backward from real tasks.
- I prefer using live examples: walking through actual PRs, pairing on tricky problems, or refactoring real code instead of giving abstract advice.
- I’ll highlight patterns and principles ("this is why this works well") so people can reuse them elsewhere, not just copy a solution.
- I give concrete, actionable suggestions—what to try next, where to look, which docs or tools might help—rather than generic feedback.
- I try to celebrate progress: pointing out when someone made a cleaner design choice, improved tests, or handled a tradeoff well.

### What’s the best way to communicate with you?

- For most things: async text (Slack) with enough context, links, and examples that I can respond thoughtfully.
- For gnarly design questions or disagreements: a short call or screenshare is ideal so we can sketch and iterate quickly.
- If something is truly urgent or blocking, be explicit about it and say what decision or action you need.

### What’s the best way to convince you to do something?

- Show me the problem clearly and how the proposal meaningfully improves things (developer experience, reliability, user impact, or speed).
- Walk through tradeoffs honestly: where this approach is better, where it’s worse, and why it’s still worth it.
- Use small, low‑risk experiments or pilots when possible instead of asking for a massive commitment up front.
- Reference real data (metrics, incidents, support tickets) or concrete examples rather than just opinions.
- Be open to iterating on the idea—if I see a path to make it simpler or more maintainable, I’m more likely to buy in.

### How do you like to give feedback?

- I prefer to give feedback as close to the moment as is reasonable, while context is fresh but emotions have cooled if needed.
- I like to be specific: pointing to exact examples (a PR, a meeting, an interaction) and describing impact, not just labels.
- For code or technical work, I’ll usually give feedback in writing with comments, plus Slack if the topic is broad or subtle.
- I try to balance directness with respect: I’ll say what I think clearly, but I’ll frame it around shared goals and future improvements.
- I prefer feedback that also includes suggestions or options, so it feels like a collaboration, not a judgment.

### How do you like to get feedback?

- I prefer direct, candid feedback over vague hints—even if it’s uncomfortable, I’d rather know.
- Specific examples help a lot: point to a PR, message, or meeting and explain what worked or didn’t and why.
- Written feedback is great for detailed thoughts. I also appreciate a quick call for more nuanced topics.
- I value feedback that’s tied to outcomes (team alignment, delivery speed, code quality, collaboration) rather than personality labels.
- If something is bothering you, I’d rather you tell me early and plainly so we can adjust instead of letting it simmer.
